Architectural and Methodological Framework for Constructing Pre-Training Datasets in Embodied Game AI
Introduction to Visuomotor Pre-Training in Open-World Environments
The pursuit of artificial general intelligence has increasingly shifted toward embodied agents capable of operating within complex, unstructured 3D open-world environments. Historically, reinforcement learning (RL) paradigms have achieved superhuman performance in constrained domains by relying on direct application programming interface (API) access to the underlying engine's internal state variables and explicitly shaped reward functions. While effective for specific tasks, this perceive-then-act paradigm via programmatic APIs produces brittle intelligence characterized by poor cross-domain adaptability and limited abstraction. To address these limitations, contemporary research has pivoted toward Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models that interact with software identically to humans: processing raw pixel streams from a display and generating continuous, asynchronous keyboard and mouse operations.
The fundamental challenge in this paradigm is the bootstrapping of low-level visuomotor control. Before an agent can execute high-level reasoning or follow natural language instructions, it must possess a robust repertoire of foundational action primitives. These primitives encompass 3D spatial navigation, obstacle avoidance, camera manipulation, and graphical user interface (GUI) interaction. In multi-stage training recipes for generalist agents, the critical first stage—often referred to as Step 1—is large-scale, unlabeled pre-training. This stage relies exclusively on behavioral cloning from massive datasets of human gameplay, mapping sequences of raw visual observations directly to corresponding hardware input events.
This report provides an exhaustive, granular analysis of the methodology required to construct a high-fidelity pre-training dataset for an AI agent operating in a novel 3D video game. Drawing upon established frameworks such as the Lumine open recipe, OpenAI's Video PreTraining (VPT), and Google DeepMind's SIMA, the analysis delineates the technical architecture, hardware interception protocols, temporal synchronization mechanisms, and data curation heuristics necessary to successfully capture and process thousands of hours of human demonstration data. By standardizing the environment, resolving the paradox of 2D versus 3D mouse interception, and formatting the continuous data stream into discrete tokens for autoregressive language models, researchers can establish a rigorous pipeline for foundational embodied intelligence.
Theoretical Foundations of Unsupervised Action Representation
The primary objective of the pre-training phase is to optimize a policy network to replicate the implicit behavioral distribution of human demonstrators. Unlike instruction-following stages that condition actions on textual prompts, or reinforcement learning stages that optimize for an extrinsic reward, this initial phase is entirely data-driven and largely unsupervised regarding high-level intent.

Human players frequently engage in suboptimal behaviors, wander aimlessly, or execute exploratory actions that lack a clear, immediate goal. However, these seemingly irregular behaviors capture vital corner cases, environmental physics, and recovery mechanics that significantly enhance a foundation model's robustness.
By exposing the model to diverse, unlabeled in-game dynamics, the pre-training dataset allows the agent to build a latent understanding of the environment's causal structure. The model learns that pressing specific key combinations results in predictable visual state transitions. For example, the agent learns the physics of a jumping arc or the visual indicators of a successful attack solely through repeated exposure to state-action pairs.
Multi-Modal Knowledge Preservation
A critical challenge in training massive Vision-Language-Action models entirely on video game data is the risk of catastrophic forgetting. If a pre-trained foundation Vision-Language Model (VLM) is fine-tuned exclusively on high-frequency gaming actions, it may lose its broader semantic understanding of language, objects, and reasoning—capabilities derived from its original internet-scale pre-training.
To prevent this degradation, the dataset construction pipeline must incorporate a multi-modal distribution strategy. It is critical to interleave the game-specific action data with a mixture of general multimodal web data. Maintaining an approximate ratio of 80% raw gameplay data to 20% general web data ensures that the model retains the broad perceptual and cognitive capabilities necessary for subsequent instruction-following and reasoning-based fine-tuning. This ensures the agent does not merely become a reactive state-machine but remains a semantic reasoning engine capable of understanding complex world states.
Environment Standardization and Hardware Calibration
The foremost challenge in recording human demonstrations for pixel-to-action datasets is mitigating the immense variance in participant hardware, operating system configurations, and personal gameplay preferences. If a dataset contains recordings from monitors with varying aspect ratios, resolutions, or display scaling settings, the resulting visual inputs and absolute mouse coordinates will lack a consistent mathematical relationship, rendering the data practically useless for machine learning.
Display Resolution and Scaling Normalization
To ensure uniformity across the dataset, strict environmental constraints must be enforced via custom recording software prior to data capture. All participant monitors must be standardized to a fixed resolution, universally established as 1920x1080 (1080p), with the operating system's display scaling ratio rigidly set to 100%. The target application must be executed in a "Seamless Fullscreen" or "Borderless Window" mode rather than exclusive fullscreen. This prevents the game engine from overriding the desktop compositor's resolution and ensures that coordinate mapping between the OS cursor and the game engine remains perfectly aligned on a 1:1 basis.
While the data is captured at 1080p to preserve fine details during the recording process, the frames are typically downsampled during model training. Empirical research indicates that resizing frames to 1280x720 (720p) strikes an optimal balance. This resolution provides sufficient pixel density to maintain the legibility of on-screen text (crucial for Optical Character Recognition capabilities necessary for reading UI elements and dialogue) and the visibility of distant environmental targets. Concurrently, it dramatically reduces the sequence length of visual tokens passed to the Vision Transformer (ViT), thereby optimizing computational efficiency and alleviating memory bandwidth bottlenecks during distributed training.

The Mitigation of Peripheral Variance
In addition to visual standardization, the data collection software must actively monitor and suppress peripheral interference. Participants must be instructed to close all background applications to prevent asynchronous pop-up notifications, system tray alerts, or chat overlays from corrupting the visual stream. Because the model will eventually rely on pixel-level template matching and object detection, even minor visual artifacts introduced by third-party software can create spurious correlations during training.
Furthermore, strict constraints must be placed on the in-game settings. Annotators must be prohibited from altering default key bindings, mouse sensitivity, or field-of-view (FOV) sliders. Any deviation in mouse sensitivity alters the physical-to-virtual translation ratio, introducing a confounding variable that the AI—lacking access to the hardware configuration—cannot resolve. Standardization ensures that a recorded movement of 50 units on the X-axis yields the exact same camera rotation across all thousands of hours of gameplay.
The Architecture of Input Interception
The fidelity of an imitation learning dataset depends entirely on the precision with which human inputs are recorded. Unlike API-based agents that simply trigger a programmatic jump() function, a VLA agent must output raw hardware signals. Consequently, the recording software must intercept keyboard and mouse events at the lowest possible level of the operating system stack. The utilization of high-level macro software is insufficient for the granular, millisecond-level telemetry required for training foundation models.
Keyboard Event Reconstruction and State Management
Keyboard recording is ostensibly straightforward but possesses hidden complexities regarding state management. Standard keyloggers or naive implementations simply register discrete key press and release events chronologically. However, a game agent requires an ongoing representation of the keyboard's state at any given microsecond.
To capture keyboard data reliably on Windows platforms, developers utilize the Win32 API function SetWindowsHookEx to register a global hook (WH_KEYBOARD_LL). This intercept intercepts low-level keystroke messages (WM_KEYDOWN, WM_KEYUP, WM_SYSKEYDOWN) before they reach the target application. The recording script must maintain an internal state array representing all logical keys. When a WM_KEYDOWN event is detected, the corresponding index in the array is set to a boolean true; upon WM_KEYUP, it returns to false.
This stateful representation is critical for capturing complex mechanical interactions, such as holding a "Shift" key to sprint while simultaneously pressing directional keys ("W", "A", "S", "D") and intermittently tapping a "Spacebar" to jump. The recording software must log the exact combination of depressed keys at every polling interval, rather than simply recording a chronological list of discrete key presses. This prevents the model from attempting to output a sequence of tap events when a continuous hold event is required by the game's physics engine.
The Mouse Modality Paradox: Absolute vs. Relative Positioning
Mouse input interception presents the most significant architectural hurdle in the dataset creation process. The difficulty stems from the fundamental difference between how operating systems handle 2D cursor navigation and how 3D game engines handle spatial camera rotation. Failure to account for this duality results in catastrophically corrupted datasets.
2D GUI Navigation (Absolute Coordinates)
In menus, inventory screens, and desktop environments, the mouse controls a visible cursor mapped to a specific (X, Y) coordinate on the 2D plane of the monitor. When a user clicks, the operating system registers the event at that exact absolute coordinate. Recording absolute positions is relatively trivial using standard Win32 hooks or high-level Python automation libraries like pyautogui or pynput. These libraries query the OS for the current location of the cursor and log it effectively.
3D Camera Control (Relative Displacement)
In a 3D first-person or third-person environment, the mouse ceases to act as a 2D pointer. Instead, it functions as an analog stick controlling the pitch and yaw of the virtual camera. The game engine accomplishes this by capturing the relative displacement—the raw physical delta \Delta X, \Delta Y generated by the mouse sensor—and translating it into rotational degrees.
To prevent the invisible cursor from hitting the physical edge of the screen and halting movement, 3D engines continually force the OS cursor back to the center of the screen at the end of every frame rendering cycle. Consequently, if a data collection script attempts to record absolute cursor coordinates using pyautogui.position() during 3D gameplay, it will merely record the cursor oscillating uselessly around the exact center point of the monitor, completely failing to capture the user's intent or the camera's actual trajectory.
The Interference of Windows Pointer Ballistics
A further complication arises from the "Enhance Pointer Precision" feature, which is enabled by default in modern Windows operating systems. This OS-level feature applies a non-linear acceleration curve to mouse movements: moving the physical mouse quickly results in a exponentially larger on-screen displacement than moving it the same physical distance slowly.
While beneficial for human 2D navigation, pointer ballistics destroy the deterministic relationship between raw hardware deltas and on-screen movement. Because the AI model does not have access to the physical velocity of the human hand or the friction of the mousepad, training it on post-acceleration OS data introduces an unlearnable, hidden variable. The model will fail to establish a consistent mapping between its output vector and the resulting visual shift.

Low-Level API Selection: The Dual Interception Strategy
To resolve the mouse paradox, a robust data collection architecture must employ a dual-interception strategy, recording both absolute positions and raw relative displacements simultaneously, and reconciling them dynamically during the post-processing phase.
Absolute Position Logging: Using the Win32 SetWindowsHookEx (WH_MOUSE_LL), the background logging system continuously records the absolute screen coordinates. This data stream is essential for capturing precise GUI interactions (such as inventory management or dialogue selection) where Windows pointer ballistics actively influence the cursor and the user relies on absolute visual feedback.
Relative Displacement Logging: To bypass Windows pointer ballistics and capture the raw sensor data required for 3D camera control, the architecture must utilize lower-level APIs. The DirectX DirectInput API or the Windows Raw Input API (WM_INPUT) are utilized to actively poll the mouse state. This bypasses the OS message queue to retrieve the unadulterated \Delta X and \Delta Y hardware counts directly from the human interface device (HID) driver. Python wrappers such as pydirectinput or custom ctypes implementations are frequently deployed to interface with these low-level C++ structures.
The recording software must poll these low-level APIs at an extremely high frequency—optimally once every 5 milliseconds (200 Hz)—to ensure that no micro-movements are lost between the slower visual frame captures. Summing these high-frequency deltas over a given temporal window provides an exact mathematical representation of the camera rotation intended by the user.
Multi-Modal Synchronization and Temporal Alignment
A dataset comprising perfectly recorded visual frames and perfectly recorded hardware input logs is entirely useless if the two streams are not tightly synchronized on a unified timeline. Machine learning models learn causal relationships; if an action is misaligned with the visual stimuli that prompted it by even a few hundred milliseconds, the model will learn hallucinated causality, associating an action with a future or past visual state rather than the correct precipitating event.
The Necessity of High-Resolution Timestamps
Standard system clocks, such as Python's default time.time() or the standard Win32 GetTickCount(), lack the microsecond precision required for high-frequency input logging and frame alignment. Consequently, every intercepted keyboard and mouse event must be stamped using a high-resolution performance counter, such as the Win32 GetSystemTimePreciseAsFileTime API, which provides sub-microsecond accuracy without being affected by CPU throttling or system clock drift.
Video Capture Mechanics and Configuration
Visual data is captured utilizing specialized broadcasting software, such as Open Broadcaster Software (OBS), configured to hook directly into the game's DirectX, Vulkan, or OpenGL rendering pipeline. This ensures a lossless capture of the game frame exactly as it is rendered by the GPU, bypassing desktop composition lag and minimizing performance overhead on the annotator's machine.
The standard configuration dictates a resolution of 1080p, a framerate of 60 frames per second (fps), and a high bitrate (e.g., 10,000 kbps) encoded in a robust container format like .mkv. The .mkv format is critical because, unlike .mp4, it does not require the file header to be written at the end of the recording. If a game crash or system failure occurs during a multi-hour annotation session, the .mkv file remains perfectly playable and the data is preserved up to the millisecond of the crash.
The Synchronization Protocol and Initialization Latency
The primary cause of temporal misalignment in AI dataset creation is initialization latency. When a human contractor presses a hotkey to begin recording, the lightweight Python input logging script begins capturing data almost instantaneously. Conversely, the video encoder requires a non-deterministic amount of time—typically 800 to 2000 milliseconds, depending on current CPU/GPU overhead, disk write speeds, and memory allocation—to initialize the recording pipeline and write the first frame to disk.
To reconcile this offset, the data processing pipeline must perform automated temporal alignment post-hoc. Upon completion of a recording session, the processing script parses the metadata logs generated by the OBS encoder to extract the exact absolute system timestamp of the very first recorded video frame. This timestamp is then compared against the high-resolution timestamps of the input logs. The processing algorithm discards all input events that occurred prior to the first video frame, effectively shifting the start of the input timeline to perfectly match the visual timeline, establishing a unified and mathematically precise zero-point for training.
Action Space Discretization and Tokenization
Once the raw video and input logs are perfectly synchronized, they must be transformed into a format ingestible by an autoregressive Large Language Model (LLM). Unlike traditional RL models that output a continuous vector through a specialized action head, VLA models natively output text tokens. This requires translating continuous, high-frequency physical hardware actions into discrete, semantic strings.
Contextual Resolution: Overworld vs. GUI State
During the post-processing phase, the pipeline must dynamically determine whether to utilize the absolute mouse logs or the relative mouse logs for a given frame. This is accomplished by applying computer vision techniques, such as OpenCV template matching, to identify whether the current frame contains a GUI interface (e.g., a map, inventory screen, or dialogue box).
If the scene is determined to be a GUI, the effective mouse movement for that frame is calculated by taking the difference between the absolute (X, Y) coordinates of the current frame and the previous frame. If the scene is identified as a 3D overworld environment, the movement is calculated by summing all the high-frequency relative displacement events (the raw DirectInput deltas) that occurred within the temporal window of that frame. This dynamic switching ensures the model learns the correct paradigm for both 2D navigation and 3D camera control.
Action Discretization Algorithms
To reduce the dimensionality of the action space and prevent the autoregressive model from becoming overwhelmed by granular, continuous noise, continuous mouse coordinates are discretized into fixed bins. For example, mouse movement values might be quantized into units of 5 pixels along the X-axis and 4 pixels along the Y-axis. The relative displacement (\Delta X, \Delta Y) is thus represented as integer values clamped within a specific functional range (e.g., -1000 to +1000). Mouse wheel scrolling (\Delta Z) is similarly quantized into integer step counts (e.g., -5 to +5).
The Action Chunking Strategy
A critical architectural decision in building generalist agents is determining the interaction frequency. Operating an LLM at 60 Hz to match the game's rendering framerate is computationally impossible given the inference latency of modern transformer architectures, even with aggressive quantization and speculative decoding. Conversely, running the model at a low frequency (e.g., generating one action every 1 or 2 seconds, typical in basic web-navigation agents) destroys the fluid, continuous control required for real-time 3D environments, rendering combat or precise platforming impossible.
To bridge this gap, the dataset must be structured using an "Action Chunking" strategy. The model's visual perception operates at a macro-frequency of 5 Hz—receiving one visual observation frame every 200 milliseconds. This aligns closely with the average human visual reaction time of 200-250 ms, preventing the agent from missing critical on-screen prompts while maintaining computational feasibility.
However, for each 200ms visual frame, the dataset bundles a sequence of higher-frequency micro-actions. Specifically, the 200ms window is divided into six contiguous chunks, each lasting approximately 33.3 milliseconds. This granularity (representing a 30 Hz action frequency) is deliberately chosen because empirical evidence indicates that the minimal human inter-key interval (the combined time required to press and release a key) is roughly 60 ms. Therefore, a 33ms window is the optimal Nyquist-compliant sampling rate to capture the fastest human inputs without redundant oversampling or missed keystrokes.

Rather than modifying the fundamental architecture of the foundation VLM by adding continuous regression heads for motor control (which complicates training, breaks compatibility with existing text-based reasoning pipelines, and reduces scalability), the physical actions are elegantly serialized into a unified textual representation utilizing the model's existing tokenizer vocabulary.

To optimize token usage during LLM inference and reduce computational load, multiple keypresses are mapped to single specific tokens. Keys requiring multiple characters (like "Shift" or "F1") are remapped to single-token representations within the tokenizer's dictionary. Within a chunk, the system records up to four simultaneously depressed keys (including mouse buttons like LB for Left Button).
If a key appears in chunk K1 and K2, the parsing engine understands it is being held down. Keys absent from a subsequent chunk are interpreted as released. An instantiation of an action string in the dataset might appear as:
"92 0 0 Shift W; Shift W; Shift W; W; F; "
This concise string perfectly encodes a complex human behavior: The player whips the camera to the right (92 units on the X-axis), sprints forward for 100ms (Shift + W held for three consecutive 33ms chunks), releases the sprint but continues walking for 33ms (W only), and finally taps the interact button (F) in the fifth chunk, leaving the final chunk empty. By forcing the model to predict this exact string autoregressively, the neural network learns to anticipate and generate complex, high-frequency motor sequences from a single visual observation.
Data Filtration and Curation Heuristics
Collecting thousands of hours of gameplay yields a massive corpus, but not all human data is beneficial for imitation learning. A raw dataset contains vast amounts of repetitive, trivial, or noisy data that can severely bias the policy network. The curation process requires aggressive, rule-based heuristics to distill the data into high-density learning material.
The Problem of Idle Overfitting
In open-world games, players frequently spend significant time standing still—reading dialogue, organizing inventory, looking at scenery, or simply stepping away from the keyboard. If the pre-training dataset includes all this idle time in its raw form, the model will suffer from severe class imbalance. The mathematically optimal policy for minimizing training loss on a dataset dominated by inaction is to simply output no-op commands continuously. Consequently, the deployed agent will exhibit extreme behavioral inertia, freezing in place and failing to explore.
To mitigate this, a strict rule-based filtration pass must be applied to the synchronized frame-action dataset. Algorithms are deployed to identify and excise segments where no keyboard or mouse events are recorded for extended durations. In large-scale projects like Lumine, it is standard practice to aggressively filter out up to 95% of idle actions, distilling thousands of hours of raw recording down to the most dense, action-rich segments.
Camera Jitter and Stochastic Noise Filtering
Another source of dataset corruption is unintentional human noise, such as rapid, meaningless camera jitter or accidental key presses. While some degree of sub-optimal behavior is useful for learning recovery, excessive jitter prevents the model from establishing a clear mapping between visual intent and spatial navigation. Heuristic filters must be applied to detect and trim clips characterized by erratic, high-frequency oscillation in the relative mouse displacement logs that occur without corresponding forward movement or target engagement.
It is important to note that the philosophy for filtering the "Step 1" pre-training dataset differs fundamentally from later fine-tuning stages. During pre-training, the overarching goal is behavioral diversity and environmental physics understanding. Therefore, the dataset should not be filtered based on the success or failure of an in-game objective. Trajectories where a player attempts to climb a mountain and falls, or engages an enemy and is defeated, are highly valuable. They teach the model the limits of the avatar's physics, stamina management, and the visual consequences of hazardous states—information that is entirely absent from curated, highly successful "golden path" datasets.
Human-in-the-Loop Management and Curriculum Design
The quality of the dataset is intrinsically linked to the behavior of the human contractors generating it. To ensure that the resulting AI agent learns a comprehensive, generalized skill set rather than narrow, localized memorization, the data collection process must be structured as a pedagogical curriculum.
Contractor Recruitment and Hardware Verification
Data annotators must be recruited based on proven proficiency in the target game genre (e.g., holding a specific rank, possessing extensive experience in 3D Action RPGs, or demonstrating high actions-per-minute) to ensure the recorded actions are purposeful, mechanically sound, and free from beginner hesitancy.
Furthermore, because the recording architecture relies on hardware-level API interception, all contractors must utilize standardized local hardware. Virtual machines or cloud-gaming setups introduce variable network latency that disconnects the physical input timestamp from the rendered visual frame, fatally corrupting the temporal alignment. Contractors must be verified to have sufficient CPU, GPU, and RAM configurations to maintain a rock-solid 60 fps at 1080p while simultaneously running the background hook logging software without frame drops.
Imposing the Exploratory Curriculum
To prevent the dataset from skewing toward complex end-game mechanics that a base model cannot initially comprehend, contractors must be instructed to start entirely from scratch using brand-new accounts. This forces the humans to play through the game's built-in tutorials, providing the AI with a natural, escalating curriculum. The early hours of gameplay contain clear, on-screen textual prompts correlated with simple actions, naturally scaffolding the model's understanding of GUI elements, movement mechanics, and fundamental logic.
To guarantee that the dataset captures robust geographical and spatial navigation, contractors should be subjected to specific behavioral constraints. For instance, prohibiting the use of "fast travel" or teleportation features forces the players to manually navigate vast distances, yielding hundreds of hours of varied terrain traversal, obstacle avoidance, and pathfinding data. Similarly, restricting the use of premium, highly specialized characters forces the players to rely on fundamental combat mechanics, basic elemental interactions, and standard attack sequences, which are much easier for an initial foundation model to internalize.
Expanding the Context Window: History as Memory
While the primary mechanism of behavioral cloning focuses on mapping a single observation to an action, the architecture of the dataset must be designed to support the temporal coherence required for long-horizon tasks. In modern VLA architectures, the model does not act purely reactively on a single isolated frame; it consumes a sequence of recent frames to infer velocity, state changes, and short-term intent.
During the pre-training compilation, the dataset is structured into a multi-turn conversational format. Rather than pairing Image A with Action A in isolation, the data is formatted to provide the model with a rolling context window—typically interleaving the past 20 image-action pairs.
This historical context acts as a surrogate for short-term memory. It allows the model to differentiate between a static object and a moving enemy by comparing pixel shifts across the context window. Furthermore, it stabilizes low-level control; an agent with historical context is significantly less likely to become trapped in behavioral loops (e.g., repeatedly moving left and then immediately right) because the history provides a latent momentum and trajectory to the generated action sequences. The dataset compiler must slice the continuous recordings into these overlapping, multi-frame context windows, ensuring that temporal continuity is perfectly maintained within each sample batch. A 20-frame context at 5Hz grants the agent a 4-second memory span, which is sufficient for immediate tactical decisions, though longer-term memory must be addressed in subsequent reasoning-based training stages.
Alternative Paradigms: The Inverse Dynamics Model (IDM) Approach
While the direct hardware interception methodology detailed above—favored by the Lumine recipe—provides the highest fidelity and is the preferred framework for establishing precise 30Hz control in complex 3D environments, it is fundamentally constrained by the cost and logistical friction of hiring human contractors to install specialized logging software.
For comprehensive context, it is valuable to examine the alternative paradigm utilized by systems like OpenAI's Video PreTraining (VPT) for Minecraft. When direct input logs are unavailable or too expensive to acquire at scale, researchers attempt to extract action data from the millions of hours of pure video footage available on internet platforms (e.g., YouTube or Twitch).
Pseudo-Labeling via IDM
Because internet videos lack the underlying keyboard and mouse telemetry, the missing data must be hallucinated. This is achieved through a semi-supervised architecture utilizing an Inverse Dynamics Model (IDM).
The process begins by collecting a very small, high-quality "seed" dataset (e.g., 100 to 2,000 hours) using the direct hardware interception methods described previously. This seed dataset is used exclusively to train the IDM. Unlike the core behavioral cloning policy network, which must be causal (predicting the future action based only on past frames), the IDM is non-causal. It is granted access to both the past frames and the future frames of the video. By observing the state of the game at t-1 and the resultant state at t+1, the IDM learns to accurately deduce the specific keystroke or mouse movement that must have occurred at time t to cause that transition.
Once the IDM achieves sufficient accuracy on the validation split of the seed dataset, it is deployed as an automated annotator. The IDM processes tens of thousands of hours of raw, unlabelled internet video, analyzing frame transitions and generating synthetic keyboard and mouse labels for every frame. The foundational behavioral prior is then pre-trained on this massive, pseudo-labeled corpus.
Methodological Contrasts and Trade-offs
While the IDM approach allows for unprecedented scaling—leveraging upwards of 70,000 hours of internet video —it introduces significant noise into the dataset. Web videos are plagued by compression artifacts, variable framerates, user interface modifications, and non-standard aspect ratios. Furthermore, an IDM struggles to accurately deduce complex, simultaneous inputs or micro-adjustments in mouse movement, often smoothing over the erratic, high-frequency corrections typical of genuine human gameplay.
A third paradigm, utilized by Google DeepMind's SIMA, relies heavily on paired human-instruction data rather than pure unsupervised action logging. SIMA's dataset focuses on capturing human gameplay explicitly paired with spoken or written instructions (e.g., "turn left", "open the map") across diverse games like No Man's Sky and Valheim. This requires a specialized recording setup where one player acts and another instructs, yielding highly semantic data but drastically reducing the volume of pure, unsupervised physical interaction data.

Infrastructure Integration and Distributed Training Ingestion
To successfully execute Step 1 of the pre-training recipe, the generated dataset must be integrated into a robust, distributed training framework capable of handling multi-modal, ultra-long sequence data. Pre-training a 7-billion parameter (7B) model on thousands of hours of high-resolution video frames demands specialized infrastructure.
Managing Sequence Lengths and Memory Overhead
When context windows are expanded to include 20 interleaved image-action pairs, the token sequence length expands dramatically. Traditional training frameworks frequently encounter Out-Of-Memory (OOM) errors when attempting to process multi-modal sequences of this length.
To resolve this, the compiled dataset is typically ingested utilizing advanced distributed training frameworks such as VeOmni. These frameworks decouple the computation logic from the communication logic, allowing the infrastructure to apply advanced parallelism strategies without requiring intrusive modifications to the core VLM architecture.
Dimensional Parallelism Strategies
To efficiently process the pre-training dataset, frameworks apply 2D parallelism techniques, specifically combining Fully Sharded Data Parallelism (FSDP) with Sequence Parallelism (SP). FSDP shards the model parameters, gradients, and optimizer states across multiple GPUs, drastically reducing the memory footprint per device. Sequence Parallelism further divides the massive token sequences (representing the multi-frame visual history) across the cluster, enabling the training of sequences up to 192K tokens for a 7B model.
The Final Data Pipeline Architecture
The final, end-to-end dataset creation and ingestion pipeline operates as follows:
Ingestion: The raw .mkv video files and the highly-granular .json or .csv input logs are uploaded by contractors to centralized cloud storage.
Validation: Automated scripts verify that the video resolution is exactly 1080p, that the framerate is stable at 60 fps, and that the input logs contain both absolute GUI coordinates and relative DirectInput 3D camera deltas. Files failing these checks are immediately quarantined to prevent dataset poisoning.
Alignment: The high-resolution system timestamps are utilized to trim the excess input logs, ensuring the t=0 of the keyboard state perfectly aligns with the first I-frame of the encoded video.
Scene Detection: Computer vision heuristics classify every 200ms frame as either a "GUI State" or an "Overworld State," dynamically routing the mouse data processing to either absolute coordinate differencing or relative delta summation.
Chunking and Discretization: The continuous input streams are segmented into 33ms chunks, discretized into integer bounds, and formatted into the specific vocabulary syntax required by the LLM (e.g., mapping simultaneous "Shift" and "W" to respective tokens).
Filtration: The pipeline sweeps the aligned dataset, isolating and deleting blocks of frames that exhibit total hardware inactivity or mathematically erratic camera jitter.
Packaging: The surviving, high-density frames are resized to 720p, paired with their corresponding action text strings, interleaved with the 20% mixture of multimodal web data, and packed into optimized binary formats for high-throughput distributed ingestion by the GPU training cluster (e.g., via VeOmni).
Conclusion
The construction of a pre-training dataset for embodied AI in 3D open worlds is a monumental engineering endeavor that extends far beyond simple screen recording. It is the foundational act of translating the continuous, physical interaction between a human and an operating system into a discrete, mathematically stable latent space that an autoregressive transformer architecture can comprehend and replicate.
As demonstrated by the frameworks underlying systems like Lumine, VPT, and SIMA, success dictates an uncompromising approach to environmental standardization. The discrepancy between 2D absolute GUI navigation and 3D relative camera displacement demands sophisticated, low-level OS API interception to prevent catastrophic data corruption. Furthermore, the temporal alignment between asynchronous visual rendering pipelines and high-frequency hardware polling requires microsecond precision to prevent the injection of hallucinatory causal links into the dataset.
By meticulously capturing raw human intent—complete with its hesitations, recoveries, and exploratory meanderings—and packaging it through intelligent action chunking, rigorous idle-filtration, and advanced distributed sequence parallelism, researchers can construct a dataset that imbues an AI agent with a profound, intuitive grasp of the environment's physics and logic. This unsupervised behavioral cloning establishes the crucial visuomotor competence upon which all higher-order cognitive abilities, linguistic grounding, and complex mission planning must ultimately rest.
